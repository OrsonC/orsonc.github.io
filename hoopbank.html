Hoop Bank

Web App for Athletic Statistics

Code found here

The Gist

My buddy and I made Hoop Bank for those who did not like the clutter of existing sport databases (such as myself). While I do not argue that there are a lot of stats that need to be expressed in the realm of sports and athletes, the core statistics of a basketball player are very rudimentary and need little embellishment. Especially in the setting of a fantasy draft, information such as the year of the player's debut or their home town hold no weight in the decision process, and as such, I wanted to create a streamlined, no-nonsense process that would lead the users to the stats as fast as possible.

UI/UX Problems

As both big fans of basketball and the NBA, my friend Alex and I follow the players and teams to keep track of how each player and team is doing. The problem with this is, the official source of data where one can track this, NBA.com, struggles with putting the barebones numbers for the stats of a player across multiple seasons, and even teams. As a result, we set forth to create a tool for those looking to find a straightforward and clean way of looking up these statistics for a player or for players of a team.

The problems that came with this was condensing a difficult process that involved a lot of information into a condensed and streamlined experience for the user.

UI/UX Solutions

To create a streamlined process for the user, a large part of the work was put into creating the database behind the web app. Creating a cohesive foundation in the information architecture was the grounds for creating an intuitive flow of information for the user to intake, and once that was figured out, I merely sought to minimise the extra, unnecessary steps in between. I start the user's process with a simple search, and then allow the teams and players to be linked to each other within a single click. The rest was simply applying a design system that would emphasize the important stats that the user wanted, and the items that could be interacted with.

Experience Flow

For our application, we sought to create a fluid process that was intuitive for users to look up statistics. In a clutch situation where fantasy drafts are on the line, nobody wants to be sitting around perusing through walls of text to find the information that they need. I wanted the experience of using the application to be simple and straightforward, allowing for ease of navigation between places that the user might want to see.

The user starts the experience at the landing page, with nothing to interact with other than the search bar. A search can be for a player or for a team, and be it the former, the user is led to the table fo stats for the player. Should the user search for the latter, or select the team on the player page they landed on, the user is led to a screen with a list of the active players on the team's roster. The two types of pages are always just a click away from each other, so as to streamline the process.

Visual Design System

Visually, I wanted the visuals to focus on the important information - the numbers. The numbers are the largest text on screen aside from the player/team name.

As type is the meat of the content, I put a lot of the emphasis on the designing on how the type was used. I selected from the red/white/blue palette of the NBA, and designated the red (#b62025) to be used for text that would link to other pages. Blue (#0062ab) is reserved for informative text, such as what a number denotes (is this "13.8" the average for the points, rebounds, or assists?) and the name of the currently searched player or team.

The Database

We designed the database schema to be one in which the user could navigate between the two types of data (players and teams) with ease. Seen here in the UML diagram, the player has an identifying ID (pid) integer, and has fields for the player's name (pname), position (position), and jersey number (number). The team has two fields, one for the team's identifying number (tid), and the other for the team's name (tname). The "player_stats_by_year," displayed within the "players" page on the app, then provides different stats for the player by displaying which pid to display for and for which tid the player's numbers were for, and having the stats show for each field (points/rebounds/assists). Under the "teams" pages on the app, the "roster" contains the field tid to know which team to display for, and a pid to display.

On the side you will find the "users" table. This table was meant expressly for admins of the console to manually insert, delete, or update existing tables within the database.

Interfacing with the databse can be done for two types of users, the admin or the general user. The general user submits a form for their search query, and is able to receive their desired information or follow links to other pages. The admin is able to update the database, either by modifying existing entries, or adding/deleting entries.
