Trubadour

Web App for Poetry

The Gist

Trubadour was designed with the disinterested younger generation in mind. As an ongoing project with Green Door Labs to inspire people to read more poetry, Trubadour was designed to suggest poems to users based off of their currently logged interests. To instill interest in poetry, which oftentimes may be deemed dull, I wanted the audience to be able to quickly find poems that may be of interest to them. This meant a quick and non-intimidating process that would lead straight to the poem, and once there, an experience that would keep them engaged within the poetry itself. As such, I created an experience that would lead to the poetry fast, and would keep the user engaged within the loop of poetry as the program continued to learn about the user.

UI/UX Problems

The trend of dwindling interest in poetry is one that has been well-documented. With Trubadour, the whole idea was to create something that would not only engage the youth (yes, I'm using "youth") with poetry and inspire them to see how cool poetry can be, but also be able to keep the users engaged with the tool on a consistent basis. Starting with only an Excel sheet with fields populated with the metadata of the poems, this task required a lot of creative nudging but also allowed for a lot of room for me to work. While there are websites that exist where collections of poems exist, none are very interactive or very inviting to users without prior interest in poetry. Trubadour seeks to provide an alternative to that.

The problems that came with this project were to create a sleek interface that would assist in getting the user to the poems they wanted to see quickly, assist in generating interest in the poetry, and to bring the users consistently back into the experience.

UI/UX Solutions

To bring the users to the poetry that they wanted as quickly as possible, I designed a process to locate poems that would be of the users' interests, and that came in the form of the selection circle. This also served to help generate interest in the poetry, but to add to that, we are currently experimenting with adding an element to gamify the poems and to make the app experience a social one to share with friends. These elements would also aid in bringing the users back to the app again, creating a healthy cycle of a network of people sharing poems with each other.

Experience Flow

Trubadour is designed to allow for an inviting and undaunting experience to ease users into the world of poetry. The experience had to require low commitment and very minimal amounts of decision making. I didn't want the users to feel intimidated by a large amount of decisions to make or have too many distractions from the experience of the poem's text itself.

The crux of the experience, I felt, should be the reading of poetry itself. Here in these user flow maps, it can be seen how central the screens with poems on them are to the overall experience.

Visual Design System

One of the most important elements to the web app's design system is the usage of color. Color is used to highlight items that can be interacted with, and is used generously throughout the app. A crucial use of color is in denoting the different ways that a user can interact with passages and poems, while the yellow highlighting of text is used to denote UI elements that the user can interact with to navigate between screens in the app. I selected a palette of bright colors with a yellow (#f9e530), green (#80c342), blue (#3bc6ef), and a red (#ed1f24) for my colors.

The selection circle is another central piece to the visual design. The user begins their poetic experience in Trubadour with a ranking of four passages select poems they resonate with. I wanted something that would be fun to interact with, even if for a simple a function as selecting passages.

To aid in the navigation of the different pages of the app, I also designed and implemented a tab system for navigation. A major part of the problem is making the multitudinous places to navigate within the tool compact and accessible. The idea was to make navigation seem deceptively simple, and so after categorizing different functions into different categories, I devised the tabbing system within the tool to make it easier to navigate what I classified as three main sections to the tool. The tabs are kept in a dock that is always visible over text, and corresponds with the section that they lead to in terms of both color and text alignment. The poem screen and tab are in an orange (#dd7f3c), the mad libs in a cerulean (#6acad7), and the social tab and screen in a pink (#cb7cb4).
