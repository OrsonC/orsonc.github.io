硬篤! Nyeh Dao!

Mobile App for Education

App prototype found here

The Gist

硬篤! Nyeh Dao! was made with a young and disinterested audience in mind. 硬篤, pronounced nyeh dao, is the term for "difficult" in my home of Taiwan's native Minnan language. The Minnan language is slowly being ousted by Mandarin Chinese in usage, and unfortunately, the Minnan language has been documented to be in decay and about to be meeting its demise within the younger generation.

Learning a language, while a valuable skill, is not always fun, especially one that is considered the language of the "older generation." Currently available resources on the language (quite understandably) feel like going through a bulky dictionary, and that tends to be something that puts people off. I sought to curate an experience that would make learning this language much more approachable to youth, and would be not only fun to look at, but also be an easy-to-navigate resource so as to not intimidate the audience.

UI/UX Problems

Inciting interest into a seemingly dead language is a task that differs based on the target audiences involved. Luckily for me, I know exactly which audiences I want to target - youth in Taiwan who have a preference for Mandarin. While there are databases of Taiwanese phrases in existence, many have yet to be ported to a digital platform, and those that have tend to be more of a reference tool rather than a tool to spark interest.

The main challenges that I was faced with was creating something without a steep entry curve that, unlike existing digital tools that serve the same purpose, could keep the user engaged.

UI/UX Solutions

Approaching this problem was a two-step process in my eye. First, I had to cause interest in the material at hand, and second, I had to maintain the interest that the user had in the app to stay and learn more.

The solution for the first issue was resolved primarily through information architecture, as sorting out a quick and easy way to access the flash cards seemed to do the trick. There was no need for an account to be made, so I removed as many steps in the process as possible between launching the app to seeing the information. There is only the process of selecting which subject matter of cards to browse through before the user gets to the cards themselves.

The second issue was a little bit more difficult to address. For this, I brought a lot of my graphic design background in to design the material look of the screens. These screens were intended to be reminiscent of flash cards, so I typography to convey the important information fast (like flash cards), while having a similar feel in interaction (flipping motion). I added a lot of color and lively emojis to further add in maintaining the interest of the intended user.

Experience Flow

I selected a collection of phrases that I feel are translated or expressed from Mandarin to Minnan in a quirky and unexpected manner. I wanted to compile phrases that were noteworthily peculiar or funny to create a stronger impression on the user so as to spark interest (no one expects the shark to be translated to "stupid fish").

Designed to be engaging and unimposing, I sought to create an environment that was easy to navigate. Instead of having specific phrases in mind, the user is offered a selection of themes in the menu, after which they are led to an assorted deck of flashcards. I kept interaction as simple as possible to decrease the difficulty in using the app, with simple icons to denote the main interactions of: read current card aloud, go to next card, and return to previous card.

Flash Card Designs

In cohesion with the intended experience behind using the app, I made sure that the basic layout of each of the phrase cards was as simple as possible. Each phrase card, though generously decorated with emojis, still placed heavy emphasis on the text itself. Outside of a strong textual element in the middle, there are a few icons used for navigation, placed in locations in relation to the central focus of the screen (the large text field in the center) that were intuitive to their function.

To draw the emphasis that the text needed, I selected a weighty font that was not obscured behind atrocious type embellishments and decorations. A heavy, non-decorative font was selected to complete the task. I put the quirky translation right in the middle with the heaviest font, forcing it to be the first thing users saw. I then placed the meaning of the phrase in Mandarin Chinese below it, and had an anglicized version of the pronunciation of the Minnan put on top.

Icon Set

As can be easily noticed, a major element of the visual design is based off a modern type of iconography called emojis. After extensive research into the great many varieties of emojis that exist in today's day and age, I selected the ones that would be relevant to the phrases I had picked, and set out to create emojis based off of what was existent. The most important thing during the creation process was making sure that the emojis would largely be confined into a square, making them much easier to handle when assembling the pattern for the background.

The icon set itself is not super extensive, and features very simple graphics to denote the various actions the user may take. All the interactive icons are limited to be in black and white to contrast from the colorful elements of the flash cards, and are in circles with a caption over them to inform of their function.
